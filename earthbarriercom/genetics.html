at Earthbarrier, we built our genetic engineering pipeline from scratch out of a scrappy little "garage science" lab in Cambridge, MA. having to build out our protocols without the support of an academic lab has been tricky, especially with just a few hundred dollars in funding while trying to do new science. but doing so has given us an unparalleled understanding of our pipeline. everything that happens at Earthbarrier happens down to the molecule, because we can't afford it not to. building the lab then from fundamental principles has allowed us to scale rapidly and effectively. putting it to the test, we're building some of our first factory cell prototypes. we're developing these organisms to generate programmable molecules as that serve as the bedrock of our climate technologies. these address myriad ecological complexities ranging from drought-mitigation and agricultural augmentation to blue carbon stimulation and direct atmospheric carbon dioxide removal.


we've mastered Bacillus subtilis and we're expanding to new model organisms. in subtilis, we've built a modular particle-generating pipeline to produce programmable nanoparticles that carry no nuclear information but are phenotypically articulated down to the atom.


as our initial usecase for this technology, we're developing solutions to sink algal species out of their photohabitable zones to augment carbon export, fight harmful algal blooms, and stimulate coastal ecosystems. our first factories are producing diatom-aggregating particles that sink diatoms into the depths and we're functionalizing them to destroy the toxins they produce on their way down.


to target biological agents of interest, we're using state-of-the-art AI to generate protein targeting domains. this allows for monovalent targeting (e.g. stitching together diatoms) or multivalent targeting, facilitating the interaction of diverse organisms. by cotargeting species of interest we can facilitate and enrich for ecological interactions that would not have necessarily taken place without us.



by targeting surface proteins of different bacteria, fungi, protists, plants, animals and any other forms of life we run into, we're building webs that are driving exciting ideas like increased soil water-retention and directed natural nitrogen fixation.
